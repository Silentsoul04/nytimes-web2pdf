<div class="l-container">

<div class="el__leafmedia el__leafmedia--sourced-paragraph">

(CNN Business)Before the pandemic, there were many reasons to be anxious
about taking an elevator — from being crammed in a small space with
strangers, to getting stuck between floors.

</div>

<div class="zn-body__paragraph speakable">

Now, as coronavirus cases exceed 18 million worldwide, many people are
concerned about catching the virus, whether from someone else in the
elevator or via the buttons.

</div>

<div class="zn-body__paragraph speakable">

Software engineer Bhavin Ahir felt the fear in the apartment block where
he lives [in
March](https://edition.cnn.com/2020/03/24/asia/india-lockdown-coronavirus-intl/index.html),
when the Indian government implemented what would become a four-month
lockdown.

</div>

<div class="ad ad--epic ad--mobile" data-ad-text="show">

<div data-ad-id="ad_rect_atf_02" data-ad-position="mobile" data-ad-refresh="default">

<div id="ad_rect_atf_02" class="ad-ad_rect_atf_02 ad-refresh-default">

</div>

</div>

</div>

<div class="zn-body__paragraph">

Ahir lives on the 12th floor of a 13-floor apartment block in the
western state of Gujarat. The tower block is home to hundreds of people
who take the elevator multiple times each day.

</div>

<div class="zn-body__paragraph">

"There is always fear to touch the buttons, so I decided to do some
developments from that side," says Ahir, the founder of Indian
electronics company, TechMax Solution.

</div>

<div class="el__embedded el__embedded--fullwidth">

<div class="zn-body__read-all">

<div class="zn-body__paragraph">

Unable to leave his apartment, he set to work in his spare room,
creating prototypes for a product now known as "Sparshless" (sparsh
means touch in Sanskrit).

</div>

<div class="zn-body__paragraph">

The system consists of a panel that is fitted alongside existing
elevator buttons. It allows users to select a floor by pointing their
finger at each button from a distance of 10 to 15 millimeters (0.4 to
0.6 inches), triggering an infrared signal which tells the elevators
where they want to go.

</div>

<div class="zn-body__paragraph">

Sparshless units are also mounted at elevator entrances on each floor,
says Ahir. Users place their hands under the arrows on the unit to
indicate whether they want to travel up or down.

</div>

<div class="zn-body__paragraph">

It's a completely contactless system designed for a world where people
have become cautious about everything they touch.

</div>

<div class="zn-body__paragraph">

### Dirtier than a toilet seat

</div>

<div class="zn-body__paragraph">

We've long known that elevator buttons are dirty.
[Studies](https://www.infectioncontroltoday.com/view/level-bacteria-elevator-buttons-40-times-higher-public-toilet-seats)
have found more bacteria on [elevator buttons than toilet
seats](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC4242253/).

</div>

<div class="zn-body__paragraph">

[Modeling shows](https://twitter.com/CorsIAQ/status/1286100808589651969)
that the risk of catching the virus from people traveling in elevators
is [relatively
low](https://twitter.com/CorsIAQ/status/1254523761790906368?s=20), as
the cars are typically well-ventilated and passengers spend a short time
inside.

</div>

<div class="zn-body__paragraph">

However, as the virus is believed to survive on some surfaces for [up
to 72
hours](https://www.nih.gov/news-events/news-releases/new-coronavirus-stable-hours-surfaces),
it's not surprising that people feel anxious about touching buttons.

</div>

<div class="zn-body__paragraph">

The pandemic has led to some creative solutions. In Thailand, a shopping
mall has [installed
pedals](https://www.channelnewsasia.com/news/asia/covid-19-coronavirus-thailand-mall-lift-foot-pedal-12753862)
so patrons can choose their floor with their feet. And in Japan, one
toothpick company is marketing "[noncontact
sticks](https://english.kyodonews.net/news/2020/05/3f0d71e4dd83-toothpick-maker-releases-button-pressing-sticks-amid-virus-fears.html)"
for pressing the buttons. In the US, the Centers for Disease Control and
Prevention recommends that people avoid touching the buttons directly
and instead ["use an object (such as a pen cap) or their
knuckle."](https://www.cdc.gov/coronavirus/2019-ncov/community/office-buildings.html)

</div>

<div class="zn-body__paragraph">

In India, Ahir sought more sophisticated technology.

</div>

<div class="el__embedded el__embedded--fullwidth">

<div class="el__image--fullwidth js__image--fullwidth">

<div>

floor.](data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhEAAJAJEAAAAAAP///////wAAACH5BAEAAAIALAAAAAAQAAkAAAIKlI+py+0Po5yUFQA7)

<div class="zn-body__paragraph">

### Making the product

</div>

<div class="zn-body__paragraph">

Ahir usually works from his company's office in the city of Surat, where
he employs 12 permanent staff. The 31-year-old entrepreneur started his
business, TechMax Solution, in 2009, straight after graduating from
college. The company's key products are security devices, but during
India's four-month lockdown, work dried up. During that time "we didn't
raise even one rupee," he says.

</div>

<div class="zn-body__paragraph">

Ahir responded to the crisis by developing the Sparshless system,
testing the first prototypes on his neighbors. Early models were
adjusted when he discovered that daylight triggered false readings. The
system also needed to be installed in such a way that it didn't affect
the elevators' normal workings — or warranty.

</div>

<div class="zn-body__paragraph">

With those problems solved, the next step was finding customers. That
hasn't been easy during a nationwide lockdown, says Ahir, but so far,
the units have been fitted in 15 buildings in India.

</div>
