<div class="el__leafmedia el__leafmedia--sourced-paragraph">

(CNN)[Mauritius](https://cnn.com/travel/article/mauritius-underwater-waterfall-must-see-attractions/index.html)
is facing a "state of environmental emergency" after a wrecked ship
began breaking up, spilling oil into the Indian Ocean.

</div>

<div class="zn-body__paragraph speakable">

The vessel ran aground at Pointe d'Esny on the east of the island nation
in late July, according to [local media
reports](https://twitter.com/Ecoaustral/status/1291982398742081536?s=20)
and environmental campaigners. This is close to the Blue Bay Marine Park
reserve and a number of popular tourist beaches.

</div>

On Friday, Prime Minister Pravind Jugnauth declared a state of
environmental emergency. Mauritius lies next to the French island of
Reunion in the Indian Ocean, and Jugnauth also appealed to French
President Emmanuel Macron for help on Twitter.

</div>

<div class="zn-body__paragraph">

The Mauritian leader
[said](https://twitter.com/PKJugnauth/status/1291780830805790721) "our
country does not have the skills and expertise to refloat stranded
ships."

On Saturday, Macron said that France was deploying teams and equipment
from Reunion Island in response.

</div>

<div class="zn-body__paragraph">

Happy Khambule, Greenpeace Africa senior climate and energy campaign
manager, warned that the bulk carrier was leaking "tonnes of diesel and
oil into the ocean," and threatening the area's wildlife.

</div>

<div class="zn-body__paragraph">

"Thousands of species around the pristine lagoons of Blue Bay, Pointe
d'Esny and Mahebourg are at risk of drowning in a sea of pollution, with
dire consequences for Mauritius' economy, food security and health,"
Khambule said in a Friday
