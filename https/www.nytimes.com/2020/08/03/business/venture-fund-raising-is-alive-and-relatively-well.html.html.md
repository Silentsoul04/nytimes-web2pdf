# Venture fund-raising is alive and (relatively) well.

</div>

<div class="css-18e8msd">

The coronavirus has made it more difficult for entrepreneurs to raise
money. But for many companies — from established businesses to smaller
start-ups — the pandemic hasn’t prevented them from [finding investors
willing to open their
wallets](https://www.nytimes.com/2020/08/03/business/dealbook/tiktok-microsoft-takeover.html).

**TransferWise** recently raised $319 million in a secondary deal, in
which existing shareholders will sell some of their holdings in the
European fintech company. [The
transaction](https://techcrunch.com/2020/07/28/transferwise-five-unicorns/)
values TransferWise at about $5 billion, a [nearly 43 percent
bump](https://www.forbes.com/sites/samshead/2019/05/22/transferwise-valued-at-35-billion-as-founders-sell-stake/)
from last year. Kristo Kaarmann, the company’s chief executive, said
that TransferWise, which was founded in 2011, didn’t *need* to raise
money, since it has been profitable for three years now. But it saw an
opportunity for older investors and long-tenured employees to sell some
of their stakes.

Smaller companies aren’t afraid to embark on fund-raising either, even
if the pandemic has changed the way they court potential investors.
**Ro**, a telehealth start-up, said last week that it had raised $200
million in new funding. **Gro Intelligence**, which uses artificial
intelligence to gather agricultural data, is in the process of raising
an “opportunistic” Series B round with a target of $50 million, its
founder and chief executive, Sara Menker, said.

It has taken time for investors to get comfortable, well, investing.
Venture deals in the second quarter in the United States, Europe and
Asia were all down from the same time last year, but up from the
previous quarter, [according to PWC and CB
Insights](https://t.yesware.com/tt/e001b3127892bff3127c5684106b4fface2be613/6d096e53b8a44a013ebee1131ad0c628/67276689dfad2ab3eddda9aeeb223346/www.cbinsights.com/research/report/venture-capital-q2-2020/).
“There’s obviously a lot more gun-shyness,” Ms. Menker of Gro
Intelligence said, with many venture capitalists initially focused on
protecting existing portfolio companies rather than striking new deals.
