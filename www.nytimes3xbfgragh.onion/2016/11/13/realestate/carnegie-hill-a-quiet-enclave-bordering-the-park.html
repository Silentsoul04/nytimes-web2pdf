Carnegie Hill: A Quiet Enclave Bordering the Park

Living in Carnegie Hill

8 Photos

View Slide Show ›

Mark Kauzlarich for The New York Times

By Aileen Jacobson

  • Nov. 9, 2016

Carnegie Hill seemed “very staid” to Neil Runyon, a 54-year-old licensed massage therapist, when he and his wife, Celeste Torello, 50, a senior director of communications for Pfizer, the pharmaceutical company, moved in 15 years ago.

“I resisted it,” Mr. Runyon said of the quiet, elegant neighborhood east of Central Park from around the mid-80s to the mid-90s. “We were still young, and it was a little bit of a culture shock.”

They had mostly been moving around in Brooklyn before they bought a two-bedroom two-and-a-half-bath duplex in a walk-up co-op building on East 93rd Street for $560,000. Soon after they moved in, their daughter, Mabel, now 14, was born.

“As it turns out, it’s a great place to live and, more important, a great place to raise a daughter,” Mr. Runyon said. “She spent her childhood in the park and in the museums.”

They live a block and a half from the park, in an area where several major museums line Fifth Avenue. Mr. Runyon walks a few blocks to his studio, Carnegie Hill Massage; Ms. Torello either walks about 40 minutes to her job on East 42nd Street or takes the subway, he said. They like several local restaurants, he said, though mostly they cook at home and read in front of their fireplace.

“What I used to think was dull is now a real oasis,” said Mr. Runyon, who lately has been supervising a facade restoration for his self-managed nine-unit building, aided by the New York Landmarks Conservancy.

Lenny Golay, 76, who with her husband, Ray Sherman, 79, owns the cozy Corner Bookstore on Madison at 93rd Street, said the tranquil residential area was “like the suburbs.” The couple, who live above the shop, bought their 1890s-era building in 1976, when the ground floor was rented to a pharmacy. “My husband had always loved the building,” she said, and they discovered it was available by chance at a cocktail party. “We paid nothing for it,” she said, declining to give the amount.

Soon after they moved in, the pharmacy went out of business, she said, so they decided to leave their jobs — she as a publisher’s representative in the Middle East, he as a model maker for architects — to open their own business and preserve the store’s wood paneling, terrazzo floor, tin ceiling and other details. Nearly half the books are for children, she said, and the adult books cater to residents who are often “very wealthy and very, very well-educated.”

She has noticed a change in the kinds of businesses in the blocks around her. “We used to have 11 or so laundries and dry cleaners,” she said. “Every one of them is now a restaurant.”

What You’ll Find

Named for Andrew Carnegie, whose 1902 mansion is now the Cooper Hewitt, Smithsonian Design Museum, Carnegie Hill, by one popular definition, runs from East 86th to 96th Street and from Fifth Avenue east to, but not including, Third Avenue. The website of the nonprofit group Carnegie Hill Neighbors, which focuses on neighborhood security and beautification, has East 98th Street as the northern boundary, but East 96th is often used, according to Joanna G. Cawley, the executive director.

The Cooper Hewitt is near two other Fifth Avenue museums, the Jewish Museum and the Solomon R. Guggenheim Museum. Madison Avenue has upscale boutiques like the Paris-based Bonpoint, for children’s clothing, but also family businesses like S. Feldman Housewares. Park Avenue, lined with co-ops, has beautifully planted green spaces called malls. Lexington Avenue’s retail includes a Best Buy and the Kitchen Arts & Letters bookstore.

Historic houses of worship dot the neighborhood, and side streets are generally quiet, with little new construction, largely because many areas have received landmark designation.

What You’ll Pay

The 185 apartments on the market on Oct. 31 ranged from $250,000 (for each of two studios) to $73.8 million (for an eight-bedroom co-op), according to Gregory J. Heym, the chief economist of Terra Holdings. In addition, 26 townhouses were on the market, ranging from $5.45 million to $34.95 million. The 138 rentals ranged from $1,800 a month (for a studio) to $85,000 (for a five-bedroom duplex).

The median sales price for an apartment was $1.65 million through September, an 11 percent jump over five years ago.

The Vibe

On school-day afternoons, children in private school uniforms roam the neighborhood’s streets, older ones on their own, younger ones with parents or caregivers. Private schools in the area include the Spence School, the Dalton School and the Horace Mann Nursery Division.

The 92nd Street Y offers cultural diversions, including classes, readings, lectures and concerts. The New York Public Library’s 96th Street Library was, naturally, one of the many libraries built with funds donated by Andrew Carnegie.

The Schools

Public School 198 Isador E. Ida Straus has 469 students in prekindergarten through Grade 5. According to the 2014-2015 School Quality Snapshot, 40 percent met state standards in English, versus 30 percent citywide, and 46 percent did in math, versus 39 percent. The school shares a building with P.S. 77 Lower Lab, a school for children identified as gifted and talented that gives priority to students in District 2, of which Carnegie Hill is part. There are 359 students; 90 percent met standards in English and 94 percent in math.

P.S. 006 Lillie D. Blake, outside the neighborhood on East 81st Street, draws students from Carnegie Hill up to East 91st Street. It has 682 students in prekindergarten through Grade 5; 75 percent met standards in English and 84 percent in math.

Some students are zoned for P.S. 290 Manhattan New School or P.S. 527 East Side School for Social Action, both also outside the neighborhood. For middle school, students are zoned for Junior High School 167 Robert F. Wagner, with 1,357 students; 57 percent met standards in English, versus 30 percent citywide, and 64 percent did in math, versus 31 percent.

Hunter College Elementary School and Hunter College High School, both for gifted students, are in the neighborhood but do not give preference to local residents.

The Commute

The 4 and 6 Lexington Avenue trains stop at East 86th Street, and the 5 does part time; the 6 also stops at East 96th, and the 4 does part time. Buses cross town at 86th and 96th Streets and run along Lexington, Madison and Fifth Avenues.

The History

Elevated railroad lines on Second and Third Avenues in the 1890s sparked the building of rowhouses on side streets between Madison and Third Avenues, according to the Carnegie Hill Neighbors website. After Andrew Carnegie moved into his mansion in 1902, more mansions, townhouses and luxury apartments followed.
