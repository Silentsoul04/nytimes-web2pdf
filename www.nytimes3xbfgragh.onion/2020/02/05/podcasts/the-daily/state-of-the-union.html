And if you take away all of the tweeting, and the things he says, and the attacking his rivals — if you took all of that away, he would be much higher in terms of approval ratings than where he is now. But where he is today is 49% in the Gallup poll. That’s pretty high for where he’s been. He doesn’t need to bring that down by reminding people that he was impeached. So he doesn’t bring it up. But at the end of the speech —

Hours after Iowa kicked off the process to choose President Trump’s 2020 opponent, and just a day before the verdict is expected in his Senate impeachment trial, the president gave his third State of the Union address. Today, we take you to The New York Times’s Washington bureau, where we examined the speech — and the unique moment in which it was delivered.

On today’s episode:

  • Maggie Haberman, who covers the White House for The New York Times.

Background reading:

  • Though Mr. Trump didn’t mention impeachment, the process hung over his address, and his refusal to shake Speaker Nancy Pelosi’s hand put the bitterness between them on full display.

  • The speech sounded like a re-election pitch, with the president claiming credit for a “great American comeback.”
