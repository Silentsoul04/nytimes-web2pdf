A Glamorous, ’70s-Style Retreat in Downtown Manhattan

The interior designer Alex P. White transformed two rooms with little natural light into a moody oasis.

By Julia Bainbridge

  • March 26, 2020

A concrete-ceilinged two-room apartment on the fourth floor of an 11-story building on Manhattan’s Lower East Side gets little natural light — very little. But this didn’t prove particularly daunting for the New York- and Los Angeles-based interior designer Alex P. White, 50, who was tasked with designing this compact space on Orchard Street last spring. White, who trained as an artist before working with the acclaimed interior designer Kelly Behun and then going out on his own three years ago, favors dark, moody lighting anyway; his Instagram page is filled with vintage photos of interiors that, unlike the bright rooms seen in home magazines today, were taken after sundown. After all, says White, “that’s when we spend the most time at home — at night.” For this project, White stuck to his druthers, making lighting a key component in what became an atmospheric oasis that recalls both the minimalism and the glamour of the ’70s.

The apartment has two main rooms — a bedroom and a living room — the latter of which is an open area consisting of a kitchen, an office space and a lounge. To reflect whatever sunshine does make its way through the living room’s sole window, White coated the opposite wall in high-gloss black lacquer. He also lined the shelving portion of a large lime-wash plaster built-in with recessed LED lights, nearly turning that bit of architecture into a light fixture itself.

The rest of the built-in — a single form with soft edges — comprises a putty-colored L-shaped platform sofa, a desk and a marble shelf wrapping from front to back. The piece is in conversation with the high-tech movement of the ’70s and early ’80s, when pioneering New York interior designers including Robert Bray, Michael Schaible and Joseph Paul D’Urso removed all traces of establishment decorating, from Oriental rugs to tasseled curtains, in favor of simplicity and functionality. Downtown industrial loft spaces were, for the first time, being reclaimed as residences, and suddenly exposed pipes and metal factory lights were considered stylish. “The idea was to break down spaces designated for different purposes, keep everything open and clean and built in, and the personality of the owner could be layered on top,” says White.

Built-ins also allowed him to tackle what he considered the real challenge of the project: storage. When Manuela Giannini, a 35-year-old designer who makes ceramic dinnerware and linen napkins under Giannini Home and will soon launch Ma Basics, a line of jeans, bodysuits and more, purchased the 846-square-foot space last spring, it had one puny closet. This would not do. For Giannini, who spends much of each year in Europe or her native Rio de Janeiro, the apartment is a home base where she keeps most of her ample wardrobe, as well as her books and objects.

White helped Giannini go through her belongings and decide which to put in an off-site storage unit; those that felt most precious, like her favorite art books and her collection of crystals, stayed with her on Orchard. He then reconfigured the apartment, turning an awkward hallway and a laundry room into a dressing area lined with built-in closets, their trimless doors set flush against the walls in order to minimize their impact. With few exceptions — an Atelier de Troupe leather sling chair, a pair of Italian high-back rattan chairs designed in the 1970s — there are hardly any furniture pieces in the apartment that aren’t built-ins. “I wanted everything to feel carved out of the space,” says White, “not applied to it.” But the apartment is far from cold. White’s creations contribute to the clean canvas upon which a few slightly louder pieces stand out all the more. In the living room, there’s a graphic abaca rug in the shape of a slithering snake. And, past the cane-clad pocket doors leading to the unapologetically glamorous bedroom, there’s leopard-print carpeting that complements a hung photograph of Giannini’s late mother wearing a leopard-print bikini.

But the real focal point — what White calls the apartment’s showstopper — is the faux fur-covered bed, another multipurpose built-in, this one designed by White and fabricated by the custom furniture designer and craftsman Angel Naula with a semicircular headboard connected to a low-slung, wraparound base that incorporates a pair of night stands. At 12 feet wide and nearly 7 feet tall, the bed wouldn’t fit into the building’s elevator, so White’s team assembled it in the room. “The right person has to come along for that,” White says of Giannini’s openness to the dramatic design, which nods to Art Deco by way of the 1980s. The direct inspiration, in fact, was a picture, featured in a 1980 Vogue article titled “A Woman’s Domain,” of Diane von Furstenberg’s master bedroom at the time. Behind Giannini’s bed is an expanse of Mokum wallpaper featuring a tropical banana-leaf motif designed by the costume designer Catherine Martin, which brings in the natural world.

The project wrapped last November, which means Giannini hasn’t been able to fully enjoy her 340-square-foot terrace accented with shou sugi ban furniture and bamboo trees. She and her two dogs have gotten very comfortable inside, however, and she looks forward to having friends sit on the bar stools at her walnut kitchen island as she pours drinks. “That’s her energy. Manuela is going to have people over, light candles and incense, turn the music up and start dancing,” says White, who upholstered the sofa cushions in a cream-and-sand-colored stain-resistant Mokum fabric. “I was just imagining the red wine.”
