    When Grief Becomes a Muscle Memory

    Text by Susan Dominus Photograph by Devin Yalkin JUNE 7, 2018

    “No one ever told me that grief felt so like fear,” C.S. Lewis wrote upon the death of his adored wife of four years. When true love first takes hold, it also feels like fear — the dawning, dizzying realization of a new level of vulnerability. “I’ve lived long enough to know that loss is often very close to love,” said Barbara Ramsey, a 78-year-old former actress. The two emotions are fresh to Ramsey, who on April 4 lost her husband of 18 years, Jack Naughton. Six weeks later, she was home in her apartment, on the Upper West Side, dealing with the arrival of a bad cold that coincided with a patch of raw spring. She walked into her husband’s closet, adjacent to her own in the bedroom, and contemplated the hundreds of ties Jack had collected since the ’50s. She pressed a button on an electric rotating rack that held them — it was a gift she gave him years earlier — and watched the collection pass before her. A few stood out: those two Liberty of London ties with small floral prints, the navy one with a recurring pattern of tiny Santa Claus faces. She spotted the black-and-silver-striped one he wore to their wedding, and then again at his 75th birthday party. She pulled those ties off the rack, for her son-in-law. It would help if they went somewhere with meaning. She did this from time to time now — just opened his closet and took in its contents, shirts and sweaters from decades earlier that he had worn beautifully for so long. It was fine clothing, meant to be worn by someone. But she would move slowly, with all of it. Even his toothbrush was still in its rightful place on the sink, still almost touching her own.

    The night before, she stayed up late, as had become her habit, watching old movies, falling asleep to the comforting, familiar sight of Ginger Rogers and Fred Astaire dancing or Jimmy Stewart being Jimmy Stewart. Then she let herself sleep late, as if trying to catch up on all the sleep she lost during the years of her husband’s illness. When she woke up, she put on one of Jack’s old dress shirts like a robe over her nightgown and tidied up. She ate a piece of toast, although she didn’t have much interest in food. She was just starting to be able to think about the good times, the theater binges in London, the vacations in Puget Sound; she found a photo of him on a visit they made to a favorite bakery on the Jersey Shore that he had loved since his early childhood.

    For Ramsey, grief is like a muscle memory: She had already lost one man she loved, her first husband, the father of her children, who was 63 when he died. When Jack died this spring, it all came back — her rote movements, her failure to focus, the sense of time stretching interminably until sleep. One loss does not instruct a person in how to better weather another one; if anything, she found that this one was summoning the pain of all the others — her parents, her first husband. That first love had four years between diagnosis and his death. When it became clear his time was ending, he gave her strict instructions on how they would spend the remainder of it. “He told me, ‘I just want you to know we are not going to do the dying part,’” Ramsey said. “‘If we do that, we have to start the day we are born. So we’re only going to do the living part. Until we don’t.’” Her voice cracked as she recalled that conversation — his intelligence, his grace.

    But strangely, nothing, not Jack’s long decline, not even her previous loss, prepared her for the shock of his death when it came — for the heaviness of his absence, the startling pain she felt when she happened on a street corner or restaurant where they shared a moment. Now the memories were hers alone, echoes where there had been communion.

    She had known Jack since 1960, at a time when she lived in the West Village and when she, her first husband, Jack and the woman he first married were all aspiring actors. They had a shared history and a shared experience: Jack lost his first spouse the same year she did. In their time together, he gave her years of unanticipated romance, years of an arm protectively wrapped around her waist, years of dancing in their living room, even long after dementia started stealing him away. They played “Fly Me to the Moon,” and he was restored, their bodies moving as they always had.

    It helped that she had a sense of what would happen to the love she and Jack shared. “Whatever my first husband gave me, what Jack gave me — I still have that,” she said. “You might lose the physical presence, but you don’t lose that.” She would not just live, she thought, but would eventually live well. “Losing reminds you of the importance of living,” she said. “That’s what we are here to do.” ♦

    Susan Dominus is a staff writer for the magazine. Devin Yalkin is a photographer who recently photographed the Arlee Warriors basketball team for the magazine.
