    Seven Boundary-Pushing Cultural Moments of 2018

    As told to JAZMINE HUGHES and MALIA WOLLAN OCT. 5, 2018

    Jenny Han on ‘Searching,’ Bo Burnham on Marvel’s Spider-Man for PS4, Jesmyn Ward on ‘Whereas’ and more.

    What Moved Me

    Jenny Han on ‘Searching,’ Bo Burnham on Marvel’s Spider-Man for PS4, Jesmyn Ward on ‘Whereas’ and more.

    As told to JAZMINE HUGHES and MALIA WOLLAN OCT. 5, 2018

    This year’s Culture Issue looks at artists who confront and redefine the conventions of who owns culture, and how to meaningfully expand those boundaries (including how to expand themselves and their own work). We surveyed several change-makers about notable inflection points in the past year — moments when they saw art that struck them as something new.

    Spike Lee’s ‘BlacKkKlansman’

    Ottessa Moshfegh is a novelist. Her most recent book, “My Year of Rest and Relaxation,” came out this year.

    The amazing thing about art is that it makes you vulnerable to truth. There are things we can do in fiction that would be intolerable in nonfiction. We can tolerate watching people being murdered or raped — I’ve been watching “The Handmaid’s Tale” with one hand over an eye. Like, I cannot believe that this exists, that people are enjoying that kind of horror. But when you’re seduced by a work of art, like a film or a book, your mind becomes open. And when the documentary footage of the Charlottesville white nationalist rally came up at the end of “BlacKkKlansman,” my heart was open. I wasn’t approaching it from clicking around the internet, weeding through everybody’s opinions. It was just this completely shocking and heartbreaking thing.

    Until then, I’d somehow forgotten that I live in a white-supremacist country. And I’m so glad to be reminded of that truth, because so much of the conversation has been like, Oh, now we’re actually talking about white privilege and then also white male privilege and the disempowerment of white males and their sob stories and women’s victimhood. I’m not trying to undermine that, but there’s a much bigger systemic problem.

    I left “BlacKkKlansman” thinking, The conversations I’ve been having about politics in America have been so dumb. I’ve spent so much time trying to avoid what’s happening in the media with gender that it has erased much more important, violent issues. It made me reflect on my own work. How political do I want to be? How directly do I want to speak to those politics? I don’t really have an answer yet.

    Zheng Bo’s ‘Pteridophilia’

    Toyin Ojih Odutola is a Nigerian-American visual artist. Her solo exhibition “To Wander Determined” appeared at the Whitney Museum from October 2017 until February this year.

    You hear it before you see it. There are these moans and grunts and you’re like, “What is going on?” And then you just arrive at it. You see a screen through the bamboo trees, and you enter the enclave, with well-hidden speakers around it. I saw it just by happenstance, walking through the botanical gardens in Palermo for the Manifesta art biennial, and I was completely transfixed by it.

    The video installation features men interacting with a forest: licking the plants, hugging them. You’re seeing these men, who are naked, so the whole thing seems very sexual, but when you start watching it — this film is about 10 minutes, maybe? — you realize that’s just the surface. It’s about this yearning that people have to connect with someone or something. In our contemporary life, there is such a distance now, or they’re a lot more guarded when it comes to opening themselves up to that kind of vulnerability.

    I’d never seen the naked male body applied in such a vulnerable way that doesn’t feel gimmicky or like it’s trying to be shocking. It’s not exploitative at all. It definitely made me reconsider how to present a naked body, because as an image-maker I deal primarily with black figures, and when people display people of color there’s often that slippery slope where that can get very exploitative. These are Asian men who are naked, and there’s nothing that is demeaning, nothing from the lens of the colonizer. It’s purely them.

    Aneesh Chaganty’s ‘Searching’

    Jenny Han is an author of young adult fiction. The film adaptation of her book “To All The Boys I’ve Loved Before” came out this year.

    The entire film plays out over screens — computer screens, cellphone screens, home-surveillance video screens. We see the entire story through the camera’s eye via home videos, texts, social media. I’ve never seen that before as a storytelling device. It’s a little disorienting at first, because you’re literally watching the cursor go across the screen. Watching it in a theater, no less, felt both intimate and like a shared community experience, as if you were a group of people watching over someone’s shoulder as they navigate online. It felt voyeuristic.

    It was also the first time I’ve seen a mainstream contemporary thriller with an Asian-American actor at the center. I saw it at the Asian American International Film Festival, so the whole crowd was mostly Asian people, which was fun because everybody was excited to see John Cho have his star moment. Nothing in the script requires him to be Asian — he just is. I would like to see more of this, stories centered on people of color where the plot isn’t predicated on them being people of color.

    I made a movie, and one of the issues of getting the movie cast and having the movie made at all was that the producers in the studios did not understand why she had to be Asian where nothing in the story required her to be Asian. Too often, when the lead is a person of color, the story is all about their struggle with their identity. It centers on their pain. Those stories are important, too, but sometimes I just want to watch a thriller or a rom-com and not have it be so weighted. As moviegoers, let us see the full range of human experience.

    Marvel’s Spider-Man for PS4

    Bo Burnham is a stand-up comic and the writer and director of the film “Eighth Grade,” which came out this year.

    It might not be very highbrow or cool, but the game has an open-world map of New York City that you can swing through as Spider-Man, and it’s truly mind-blowing. You can go uptown or swing around Central Park. I stumbled on Washington Square Park, where I spent so much of my late teens and early 20s. That was a very emotional moment. I couldn’t believe how real everything looked. Or you can climb to the top of the Empire State Building or the Flatiron Building.

    I’ve brought over older friends and friends who don’t play video games just to show them how incredibly beautiful video games can be. They used to be like an imitation of an experience that was fantastical, and now they are the fantastical experience. A lot of people who don’t play games don’t realize that the artistic endeavor of a video game is better than any blockbuster movie. And because video games are a less-straightforward narrative, they’re often able to be artistically riskier than a $200 million film.

    Part of the appeal of games is that at first your jaw is on the floor because you’re swinging around and going, “Oh, my God, I’m Spider-Man!” And then two weeks later, you’re playing it, and you’re like, “Yeah, I’m Spider-Man, who cares?” That’s almost what you’re pursuing, that competency that goes hand in hand with jadedness. There was a Spider-Man game when I was a kid — I thought it could never get cooler than that. It did. I hope it never gets cooler than this, because if it does, we’re just going to be plugged in and getting fed through an IV.

    Layli Long Soldier’s ‘Whereas’

    Jesmyn Ward is a novelist and 2017 MacArthur Fellow. Her most recent book, “Sing, Unburied, Sing,” won the 2017 National Book Award for fiction.

    I recently read this collection because it won a National Book Critics Circle Award this year. There’s a poem called “He Sapa,” and the second section — it’s like 11 lines or something — is about the word “drag.” She unpeels that word and paints this really vivid picture of a man who’s been violently dragged, a Native American who has been killed. How his hair would be loose, and how his shirt would have snaps, and they’re mother-of-pearl, and how the dragging begins with a yank and a slide. It’s so specific, so unrelenting in its definition of what it means to drag someone — to enact this violence on someone else.

    The book is a response to a congressional apology to Native Americans, where the meaning behind the words wasn’t clear. She is unpacking that language and unveiling the truth beneath it, the history and behavior on the part of the United States government. There’s this tendency by those powers that be to obfuscate meaning and to take power away from language by robbing it of its truth. One reason this book struck me so much is because she’s directly speaking against that.

    Her book and that poem, in particular, reminded me what careful language can do. It made me recommit to writing — I’m really working into the past in the novel I’m working on now because I’m writing about slavery, which has been really difficult for me to do — and made me believe again in the power of writing and the truths that it can reveal for people and what that remembering and honoring the truth can do for the individual, but also for the group, for all of us.

    Seth Price’s ‘Danny, Mila, Hannah, Ariana, Bob, Brad’

    Taryn Simon is a multidisciplinary artist. She was a 2001 Guggenheim fellow.

    Seth Price’s recent exhibition at MoMA PS1 is a series of abstract portraits of magnified details of human skin in superhigh resolution. He used a robotic camera system that’s usually employed for scientific research and took thousands of images of a body part and stitched them together with satellite-imaging software. They’re printed on fabric and stretched over light boxes, so they’re almost like advertisements to us of what we are. It felt like seeing the tools of photography and documentation working almost too well. They’re cataloging and processing our most intimate and most unknowable selves, and the cellular detail of who we are, and where we come from, and what that means was just overwhelming.

    Even though the cameras are hyperclose on the humans, they somehow render a completely foreign space. I was just reading about 3-D photographs that can capture dynamic images of proteins at a single-atom scale — and thinking about how we’re also at a time that seems to have lost its taste for precision. The work just rode that line for me. Price used extreme technologies, but to no end beyond making us feel something.

    Large-format photography has always traditionally been understood as being able to see more than the eye can see, and this exhibition felt as if it was that, but on steroids. In a way, the more precise the camera has become, the more otherworldly the images and the psychological space it renders. The further you go in that direction, the stranger and more fantastical it all becomes. The experience makes you feel that you’ve entered another universe.

    Cristina Gallego and Ciro Guerra’s ‘Birds of Passage’

    Damien Chazelle is a screenwriter and an Oscar-winning director. His next movie, “First Man,” comes out in October.

    The film begins as a variation on an ancient tale: You’re in this tiny Colombian village, and a man wants to wed a woman he loves, but he doesn’t have the dowry. Slowly he stumbles into the drug trade. You’re not even entirely sure what era this is; it all feels somewhat timeless. The very first time you see a car, maybe 20 minutes or half an hour in, is almost a shocking moment — it’s an emblem of modernity encroaching on the village. There’s this initial cognitive dissonance about the world that you think you’re in. It’s a like a frog being boiled in water and not realizing that the water is being turned up. By the end, you’re in a full-fledged, and quite violent, mob movie, but that transition of storytelling — from one world to the other — is done so methodically and elegantly that it never feels like a schism. It feels like a natural, organic unfolding of the story.

    I don’t think the movie would work half as well if it didn’t take the time in the beginning to live in the details and the texture — almost in a documentary way — of the world it depicts. You become almost like an anthropologist: You’re examining this culture and these rituals before you really know what the narrative is going to be. It’s a courageous thing to do as a filmmaker, because there’s often this impulse that you want to set your gambit right away, but this film is brave enough to let that happen slowly.
