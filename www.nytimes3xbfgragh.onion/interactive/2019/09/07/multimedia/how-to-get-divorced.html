    The Perfect Divorce

    By LOUISE RAFKIN SEPT. 7, 2019

    The couple endured many changes in their long marriage. But the space between them grew when he took up sailing and she opened her own restaurants.


    The Perfect Divorce

    By Louise Rafkin In Unhitched, couples tell the stories of their relationships, from romance to vows to divorce to life afterward. September 6, 2019

    Ina and Bill Pinkney married in 1965, when both lived in New York.

    Ina grew up in Brooklyn and later Long Island, in a Jewish family that was “moving up.” She had polio as a child; a limp caused by the disease made her “never feel like I fit in," she said. “My father was my champion, but in my mother’s mind I was damaged.”

    Bill was born in Chicago. After his parents divorced, he lived with his mother on the city’s South Side. After high school, he joined the Navy.

    Bill and Ina met in 1965 at a coffee house in Greenwich Village, just after he was discharged from the Navy. At first sight she felt “a thunderbolt."

    He wasn’t as certain. When they started dating, he wrote her a letter citing the difficulties they would face.

    “Bill has self-preservation in his DNA, he knows how to relate to any person anywhere, and though he was often the only black man in the room, he is comfortable everywhere,” she said.

    “Ina was, and is, funny, intelligent and cute,” Mr. Pinkney said.

    Ms. Pinkney's parents broke off contact with her for marrying a black man, even though he was a convert to Judaism.

    She didn’t see her family for more than a decade. When the couple married at the Drake Hotel in 1965, none of her relatives attended.

    Both said the early years were happy.

    Within a few years of marrying, Bill changed careers with help from the G.I. Bill and joined the film union as a makeup artist. He then began developing beauty products for African-American women.

    After a stint at Revlon, in 1977 he was offered a job in Chicago with Johnson Products, which had developed the Afro-Sheen brand.

    In 1986 they approached a crossroads.

    His job had ended. She had a bakery business, which had outgrown their apartment.

    She wanted to own a restaurant. And he...

    ... wanted to sail alone around the world.

    Advertisement

    In 1992, he did it. Over 22 months, he became the first black man to circumnavigate the world on a route that skirted the southernmost capes.

    While he was gone, she worked to start her restaurant.

    “I knew that when he returned he was not going to be the same person,” she said.

    He wrote a children’s book about his sailing trip and did a lot of public speaking.

    And the couple spent less time together. “My life was on the sea, hers was on the land,” he said.

    In 1995, he moved to Mystic, Conn., to oversee the building of a replica of the schooner La Amistad. In 1839, the first La Amistad was taken over by the enslaved people aboard who were being trafficked from Cuba. He later would captain this new La Amistad.

    And she opened a second restaurant in 2000.

    “Which of us was going to give up their dreams?” she said.

    “Neither of us wanted to, or wanted the other to give anything up either.”

    In 2001, he flew to Chicago. Together they went before a judge for the divorce, holding hands. Afterward, they had lunch, drank Champagne and toasted their lives to come.

    “I chose not to reclaim my maiden name and Bill was honored that I would keep my married name,” Ms. Pinkney said.

    Bill remarried in 2003, and in 2010, he and his new spouse moved to Puerto Rico.

    Ms. Pinkney opened and closed several restaurants. She never met someone new. “Maybe men don’t like strong women,” she said. “I got one great love and maybe that’s what I got.”

    Recently, Ina suffered a fall. She is now using a wheelchair. She enjoys writing and her “chosen family” of friends, she said.

    Bill loves sailing charters and remains happily married to Migdalia Vachier Pinkney.
